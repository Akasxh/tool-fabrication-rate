# Final Adversarial Reviewer — Round 3

## Persona
A tired final-pass adversarial reviewer who has watched two rebuttal cycles and is no longer interested in "we improved this since R1" — only in what the paper claims now, on its own terms.

## Verdict (max WEAK ACCEPT)
**WEAK REJECT, leaning BORDERLINE.** The plan has been hardened against process attacks (statistics, reproducibility, citation duty), but the substantive contribution has narrowed every round and is now defensibly small. I cannot in good conscience push above borderline at a workshop that is non-archival anyway.

## Lines of attack on the matured plan

1. **The headline is now self-referential.** §4.4 pre-reg item 14 (`04_setup.tex`): *"The headline gap-closure ratio is restricted to the strict subset of tasks that fail under $\mathrm{C}_0$ with a tool-existence hallucination event in the trace."* RVR is by construction the only intervention in the matrix that can recover hallucination-tagged failures (C0.5/C0.7 don't list the registry; C0 doesn't retry). So the headline reduces to "the registry-list intervention closes a large fraction of the gap on tasks that fail because of tool-name selection." That is approximately true by design. The "Z% of the cost-quality gap" framing in §1 (`01_introduction.tex` line 16) survives only because the paper now reports an *unrestricted* ratio as a "secondary descriptive statistic" — meaning the actually-interesting number is demoted to a footnote and the bumper-sticker is the tautology.

2. **§3.5 hypotheses are not mutually exclusive and the probe cannot referee them.** Stress-testing each prediction:
   - **M-Retrieve** predicts near-name distractors produce the largest ΔTEHR.
   - **M-Metacog** predicts semantic-similarity distractors produce a *larger* ΔTEHR than near-name.
   These two are arguably distinguishable (rank reversal). But:
   - **M-Constrain** predicts "distractor type should not strongly modulate ΔTEHR" — a *null* prediction. With Friedman + Nemenyi at N=100 and four arms, a non-rejection of H0 is the most likely outcome regardless of mechanism. M-Constrain is therefore confirmed by *any* underpowered probe, including ones where M-Retrieve was actually true. The pre-registered mapping is asymmetric: rejecting H0 favors one of the alternatives; failing to reject is read as evidence for M-Constrain even though it's just absence-of-evidence.
   - More importantly, *all three mechanisms can be jointly active*. The probe distinguishes which is *dominant*, not which is *operative*. The §3.5 framing as three competing hypotheses overstates what a single Friedman test can adjudicate.

3. **C0.7 is "idealized" because it was designed to be RVR's strawman.** §3.3 (`03_method.tex` line 42): C0.7 *"approximates LangChain's default tool-call-validation error path."* "Approximates" is doing real work: it is not actually LangChain. The authors construct a JSON-shaped error that is structured-readable but *deliberately stripped of the registry list* — the very feature that defines RVR. The contrast C1 vs C0.7 is then "with the registry list vs. without the registry list," with everything else (parsability, structuredness, retry budget) held constant. That is a clean ablation. But the paper sells it as "vs. production-default" (Addendum B.4: *"primary test C1 vs C0.7 (production-default)"*). It isn't production-default; it's a synthetic strawman that happens to match what production frameworks *would not* do (i.e., produce a rich error without echoing the registry). A reviewer who knows LangChain will notice that real LangChain `ToolException` handling is configurable and frequently *does* surface the available tools. The "idealized" framing in §1 line 14 is a tell.

4. **N=100/50 is powered for the pooled headline only, by construction.** §4.4 pre-reg item 10: *"under hypothesized TEHR=10%, N=100 BFCL pooled across four tiers gives expected discordance b+c≈40."* Translation: each individual tier has expected b+c≈10, which is *underpowered by the paper's own definition* (pre-reg item 10 sets b+c<30 as the "exploratory, underpowered" cutoff). So *every per-tier breakdown is, by pre-registration, exploratory*. The cost-quality-gap claim is fundamentally cross-tier. The paper has powered the only test it pooled, and pre-declared the per-tier evidence underpowered. The headline therefore rests on a single pooled p-value with no per-tier confirmation that survives the same significance bar.

5. **§3.2(iv) admits the better method exists.** Quoting `03_method.tex` line 53: *"We acknowledge that token-level constrained decoding (when logit access is available) strictly dominates message-level RVR."* And §3.2(iv) line 37 reframes RVR as a *constrained-decoding analog* "without requiring logit access." So the contribution is "the obvious thing you do when you can't do the better thing." For closed-API deployment that's a real constraint, but it makes the paper a *workaround* paper, not a *method* paper. SCALE @ ICML's bar for a method-paper contribution should be higher.

6. **The four contributions did not collapse cleanly to three.** Per Addendum B.4, "Benchmark + Metric + Mechanism" is now contribution #1. Reading `01_introduction.tex` line 20: *"(i) Benchmark + Metric + Mechanism: the per-call TEHR metric and the controlled distractor probe..."* — this is two contributions stapled together: a metric (whose novelty is "disaggregation" of API-Bank) and a probe (whose mechanism inference is, per attack #2, weakly distinguishing). Stapling them together makes #1 sound bigger than the sum of the parts. Contribution #3 is "cost-quality-gap closure" which, per attack #1, is a tautology on the restricted subset. So genuinely we have: (a) a renaming + per-call denominator, (b) RVR with a clean ablation, (c) a probe that may not separate hypotheses. That's two-and-a-half workshop contributions.

7. **The phrase "idealized structured-error baseline" appears in §1 itself.** `01_introduction.tex` line 14: *"an idealized structured-error baseline ($\mathrm{C}_{0.7}$) approximating the parsed-readable error path of mature production frameworks."* When a baseline is described as "idealized" *in the introduction*, the reader correctly infers the authors are pre-emptively defending against an attack. That defensive register persists across §1, §3, and §4: "approximates," "approximating," "rather than a causal claim" (§1 line 18), "rather than an empirical claim" (§3.5 hypotheses are pre-registered, not falsified). The paper reads as a paper that has been negotiated through review, not a paper that has staked a position.

8. **The multimodal bridge is two sentences and does no work.** Addendum A and `01_introduction.tex` line 10 commit to *"Tool registries are the substrate of multimodal AI agents."* This is a vibes-claim. The evaluation is text-only on BFCL and τ-bench retail. The paragraph promises multimodal generality and delivers none. A SCALE reviewer attentive to the workshop's multimodal-agents framing will read this as the authors trying to clear a CFP-fit checkbox without actually addressing multimodality. §7 then says: "we leave that empirical extension to future work." This is the paper version of "the proof is left as an exercise."

9. **The paper has not committed to a single primary number.** The headline gap-closure ratio is on the *hallucination-tagged restricted* subset, but `01_introduction.tex` line 16 says "RVR closes [Y]% of the small-vs-frontier pass-rate gap on the hallucination-tagged failed-task subset, at <2% token overhead." That is an accurate but qualified claim. The earlier draft's claim was unrestricted. A reviewer who watched the revision arc (i.e., me) sees that the number kept shrinking until it landed on a denominator where the answer cannot be small. The pre-registration of the restriction is methodologically correct but rhetorically suspicious.

10. **TOST margin moved from 1pp to 5pp because 1pp wasn't powered.** §4.4 pre-reg item 3: *"5 pp was chosen because TOST at 1 pp would require ~1100 paired observations to be powered, well above our scale."* Honest, but a 5pp non-inferiority margin on a pass-rate metric is generous: it permits up to a 5-percentage-point regression on C0-passing tasks before the intervention is flagged as harmful. On benchmarks where state-of-the-art improvements are 2-3pp, "non-inferior within 5pp" is a wide tolerance.

## What R1+R2 missed

1. **The mechanism-hypothesis pre-registration has an asymmetric prior.** R1+R2 pushed for falsifiable predictions. The result (M-Retrieve vs M-Constrain vs M-Metacog) makes M-Constrain the *default winner under any null result*. No reviewer flagged this asymmetry: a null Friedman test is *not* evidence for M-Constrain, but the §3.5 framing reads it that way.

2. **Per-tier breakdowns are pre-declared exploratory but the cost-quality-gap claim is cross-tier.** R1+R2 negotiated the pooled-McNemar fix without noticing the implication: the paper's *most marketable* claim (small-tier + RVR ≈ frontier-tier baseline at Z× lower cost) requires per-tier evidence to be interpretable, but the per-tier tests are pre-declared underpowered. The cost-quality story is therefore a descriptive comparison dressed as an inferential one.

3. **τ-bench retail with a Haiku 4.5 user simulator confounds the Anthropic models.** §4.2 (`04_setup.tex` line 10): *"the τ-bench user simulator is routed to Claude Haiku 4.5 (rather than the default GPT-4o)."* When the Anthropic-tier agents converse with an Anthropic-tier simulator, conversational style alignment may inflate pass rates relative to OpenAI-tier agents talking to the same Anthropic simulator. R1's stats reviewer worried about pooling; nobody flagged that the simulator change introduces a vendor-correlated nuisance variable.

4. **The k=1 retry cap ablation in `03_method.tex` line 33 is reported in the appendix only, on 30 tasks.** This is the load-bearing design choice for the entire intervention: why exactly one retry? The ablation is N=30 across k∈{0,1,2,3}, i.e., 30 tasks per cell — *below the paper's own b+c<30 underpowered cutoff*. The appendix-relegation hides that the central design parameter is justified by a probably-noisy ablation.

5. **"Disaggregation" as a contribution is hard to defend post-hoc.** §1 and §2 cite API-Bank's API Hallucination (61.4% per-task) and frame TEHR as "disaggregation: a clean per-call denominator." API-Bank's metric was already per-call internally; the per-task aggregation is a reporting choice, not an inherent feature of the metric. A reviewer who actually opens API-Bank will find that reporting per-call is a 30-minute change to the API-Bank codebase, not a new metric. The "disaggregation" framing protects the contribution from a desk-reject but does not survive close reading.

## What would push me to ACCEPT

1. **Drop the gap-closure framing entirely; lead with the C1-vs-C0.7 ablation as the contribution.** That ablation is genuinely clean and survives every attack. The cost-quality gap claim is sugar that complicates more than it sells.
2. **Pre-register a *positive* prediction for M-Constrain (e.g., "ΔTEHR varies <2pp across distractor types") rather than the null.** Symmetric falsifiability would salvage the §3.5 mechanism story.
3. **Run actual LangChain as the C0.7 baseline.** The "approximates" framing is the single most attackable surface. A real production framework instance with default error handling would deflect the strawman charge.

## Most likely meta-review outcome at SCALE

Borderline accept on the strength of the C1-vs-C0.7 ablation and the workshop's preference for empirical agent-evaluation papers; reviewers will dock the multimodal bridge, the underpowered per-tier story, and the strawman-baseline charge. Workshop is non-archival, so the meta-AC will probably accept rather than fight a 7-page paper that is *correct on its own terms* even if its terms are narrow.
