# Aggressive Skeptic — Reject Argument

## Verdict
**REJECT.** Confidence 4/5. Workshop-tier null-result paper repackaging a failed positive-result hypothesis as an "audit." Recommend the authors either rewrite as a tools/infrastructure paper or rerun on a regime where TEHR > 0.

## Rejection rationale (3 paragraphs)

**The paper is a salvage operation.** The infrastructure-to-finding ratio is grotesquely lopsided: a 5,300-LOC harness with 144 unit tests, a four-condition ablation ladder ($\mathrm{C}_0/\mathrm{C}_{0.5}/\mathrm{C}_{0.7}/\mathrm{C}_1$), a paired McNemar primary test, BCa cluster-bootstrap CIs, a Friedman omnibus with pre-registered asymmetric falsifiability, and a four-distractor Czapla-style probe — *all to report that 0/1571 calls hallucinated*. No one builds that machinery to confirm a null. The intervention (RVR) has a "design, harness, and pre-registered evaluation protocol" but the authors openly admit they "cannot demonstrate RVR's recovery effect within this submission's envelope" (abstract, lines 58–59). What we are reading is the residue of a paper that started life as "RVR fixes tool hallucination" and pivoted to "we audit the claim and find it's not a problem" once $\Delta$TEHR was undefined. The pivot is not declared.

**The "rare phenomenon" claim is internally inconsistent.** §1 line 47 acknowledges that BFCL provides a "redundant tool basket" so removal "can be worked around via decomposition rather than fabrication," and §7 line 30 acknowledges that the Czapla scenario is specifically about ambient runtime functions reachable beyond the registry — a configuration "that BFCL's strictly-typed Python-class executor cannot emulate" (§5 line 127). The paper therefore admits its evaluation is structurally incapable of producing the failure mode it claims to audit, and yet the title still asserts "Tool-Existence Hallucination Is Rarer Than Reported" and the abstract still asserts "TEHR is exactly $0$ in every condition tested." Pick one: either you tested the regime and found it rare, or your benchmark cannot test the regime. Asserting both is a tautology dressed as a finding.

**The generalization claims are unsupported by the sample.** Two models, one vendor (Anthropic), $22 USD of compute. The abstract's phrase "modern Anthropic frontier and small models simply do not fabricate tool names" (§1 line 32) becomes, in the title, "rarer than reported" — full stop, no vendor qualifier. Anthropic models are heavily trained on tool use; this is a single-vendor convenience sample, not a regime audit. §7 line 11 dutifully lists every vendor we did not test (Gemini, Llama, Mistral, DeepSeek), then the abstract and title generalize past that limitation. The combination of (a) a benchmark that cannot exhibit the failure, (b) a single-vendor sample biased toward tool-use hardening, and (c) a $22 budget gives us no statistical purchase on the population claim. This is a workshop note at best, and it's framed as a recalibration.

## Lines of attack (12)

1. **The pivot is undeclared.** The harness has "RVR + 4-condition + paired McNemar implementation, none of which is informative without TEHR > 0" (§5 line 114: "RVR's recovery effect can only be measured on a non-empty subset of $\mathrm{C}_0$-failed-with-hallucination tasks"). No one pre-registers a McNemar test for an outcome they expect to be all zeros. The paper was a positive-result paper that became a null-result paper after data collection, and the framing should declare that pivot. It does not.

2. **The "rare" claim is a tautology given the chosen evaluation.** §1 line 47 ("redundant tool basket") and §7 line 30 ("BFCL's strictly-typed Python-class executor cannot emulate" the ambient-function configuration) both acknowledge that the benchmark cannot exhibit Czapla's scenario. The paper then uses this as both *the reason the failure didn't surface* and *evidence that the failure is rare*. You cannot have both — one is a measurement limitation, the other is a population claim.

3. **Two models, one vendor.** §1 line 76 calls this "two Anthropic capability tiers" but the title and §1 line 22 ("we find the opposite") generalize. Anthropic explicitly invests in tool-use post-training; this is not a representative sample of "modern frontier models." Where is GPT-4.1, Llama-3.3-70B, Gemini 2.5, Mistral Large? §7 line 11 notes the gap; the abstract ignores it.

4. **$22 USD is not frugality, it is insufficient scale.** With 1,571 calls and 0 events, the upper 95% CI for TEHR is approximately $1 - 0.05^{1/1571} \approx 0.0019$, which is informative for the *aggregate*, not for any per-condition cell. The probe cells of $N{=}122{-}143$ have one-sided 95% upper bounds around 2–3 percentage points. A $25\%$ MetaTool-style effect would be detected; a $1\%$ effect would not. The authors claim near-zero across all conditions, but the per-cell power is too low to support per-condition conclusions. A 100x budget would either surface TEHR in some cell or genuinely prove its absence; this budget does neither.

5. **MetaTool comparison is loose.** §1 lines 41–43 call this "an analogous protocol" but MetaTool's Sub-task 3 is single-turn tool-removal with a different scoring rubric and registry composition. BFCL's `multi_turn_miss_func` differs in turn count, registry size, redundancy structure, and outcome adjudication. The paper claims "$25\%$ on 2023 models, indistinguishable from zero on 2026 models" as a recalibration; without a tight replication of MetaTool's exact protocol on 2026 models, the $25\% \to 0\%$ delta is confounded by every dimension on which the protocols differ. This is the headline contrast in the introduction and it is not earned.

6. **§6 "consistent with M-Constrain but underpowered" is meaningless.** §5 lines 84–92: the Friedman omnibus on per-task TEHR is $\chi^2 \approx 0$, $p \approx 1.0$ because *every cell is zero*. A degenerate test on all-zero data carries no information about distractor-type modulation. The paper's pre-registered asymmetric falsifiability rule ("non-significant *and* power $\geq 0.80$") protects the authors from claiming positive support, but the result is reported as "consistent with M-Constrain" anyway. It is consistent with *every* mechanism hypothesis simultaneously, because the data discriminates among none of them. Reporting this as a mechanism finding is misleading.

7. **The "release the harness" framing is a hedge.** Contribution (iv) (§1 lines 94–98) is "an open-source experimental harness." Contribution (iii) (§1 lines 82–93) is RVR with a protocol the authors "cannot demonstrate within this envelope." If the harness is the contribution, write a tools/infrastructure paper; if the regime audit is the contribution, validate the regime claim on a regime that actually exhibits the failure. Doing neither cleanly produces a paper that is two half-papers stapled together.

8. **The title overclaims.** "Tool-Existence Hallucination Is Rarer Than Reported." Rarer than reported *where*? Czapla is a production observation; MetaTool is on 2023 models; BFCL multi-turn is a different distribution from both. The paper compares 2026 Anthropic models on BFCL to 2023 vendor-mixed models on MetaTool to 2025+ production traces on Czapla — three different (model, benchmark, regime) configurations — and concludes "rarer." That is a comparison across three uncontrolled axes simultaneously.

9. **Haiku 4.5 was only tested on the probe.** §5 line 22: "Haiku~4.5 was evaluated only on the distractor probe due to compute budget." So three of the six "regime tests" are Sonnet-only. The contribution claim of "two Anthropic capability tiers" (§1 line 76) is therefore a one-tier audit on the BFCL splits. The phrase "across two Anthropic models and six regime tests" in §5 line 147 obscures this asymmetry.

10. **The contribution count is inflated.** Four contributions are claimed; (i) the diagnostic is a Boolean check on a parsed name against a set; (ii) the regime test is a null result on a benchmark that structurally cannot exhibit the failure; (iii) RVR is unvalidated; (iv) is a harness. A workshop should accept two of these on a generous reading. The paper's framing of (ii) as "the first systematic null-result audit" (§1 line 79) does heavy rhetorical lifting for a finding that is partially baked into the benchmark choice.

11. **Token-overhead non-claim.** §5 lines 129–142 reports overhead "well-defined" with "$<5$ tokens" added to the re-prompt. But the recovery effect is undefined. So we have a precise cost figure for an intervention with an unmeasured benefit. This is the classic "we measured the easy thing because the hard thing didn't fire" structure; it should not appear in the results section as a top-level subsection.

12. **No power analysis on the headline claim.** The abstract says "TEHR is exactly $0$" — but with $N{=}81$ on long_context, the Rule of Three gives an upper 95% CI of $\sim$3.7%. The paper does not state per-cell upper bounds anywhere I can find in §5. Reporting "exactly 0" without the corresponding upper-bound framing is a misuse of point estimates from small samples.

## What would change my mind

**Priority 1: Run the experiment on a regime where the failure can occur.** Either (a) a custom registry that exposes ambient runtime functions à la Czapla, or (b) at least one weaker open-source model (Llama-3.1-8B, Mistral-7B, Qwen2.5-7B) where tool-use hardening is plausibly weaker. If TEHR > 0 surfaces in even one such cell, the paper's regime-dependence claim becomes empirical rather than negated-by-construction. Without this, the audit is a tautology.

**Priority 2: Tighten the MetaTool comparison.** Replicate MetaTool Sub-task 3's exact protocol (registry, turn structure, scoring rubric) on the same Anthropic 2026 models. If $25\% \to 0\%$ holds under matched conditions, the headline is earned. If it shrinks to e.g. $25\% \to 8\%$, the paper has a quantitative recalibration finding rather than an absolute denial. Either is publishable; the current looseness is not.

**Priority 3: Drop one of the two papers.** Either (a) cut RVR entirely, retitle as "A null-result audit of tool-existence hallucination on BFCL multi-turn with current Anthropic models," and lean into the regime-test framing honestly with per-cell upper bounds in the abstract; or (b) cut the audit framing, retitle as "Registry-Visible Reprompting: design, harness, and pre-registered evaluation protocol," and submit it as a tools/infrastructure paper without claiming a regime finding the data cannot support.

## Predicted meta-review outcome

**Will my rejection survive AC averaging?** Probably not, in a workshop. SCALE workshops weight infrastructure contributions and "useful negative signal" generously, and the paper's pre-registration discipline, harness quality, and statistical care will land well with reproducibility-minded reviewers (R1_reproducibility, R3_consistency_audit are probably positive). The synthesis reviews already in PHASE0/REVIEWS suggest a borderline accept. My rejection contributes weight rather than decisiveness: the AC averages a 2 (mine) with a likely 5/4/3 from the others and produces a 3.5 — a borderline accept at a workshop, borderline reject at a main conference. 

The realistic outcome is **conditional accept with a "tighten the framing" mandate**: drop the "rarer than reported" title to "regime-dependence of tool-existence hallucination," demote the abstract's generalization claim to a vendor-conditional claim, add per-cell upper bounds, and ship the harness. My core attacks (#1 pivot, #2 tautology, #3 single-vendor, #5 MetaTool looseness) survive as required revisions even if the paper is accepted; that is the *useful negative signal* the workshop format is supposed to extract. Worth writing the review even when it loses the vote.
