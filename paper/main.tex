\begin{abstract}
Tool-name hallucination is a known failure mode, but who does it, how
often, and what fixes it? We define a per-call Tool Fabrication Rate
(TFR) and measure it on BFCL multi-turn. Commercial frontiers are silent.
Across $6{,}000{+}$ opaque-baseline calls (five Anthropic 4.x versions at
$0/2{,}592$, $\leq\!0.14\%$, and the OpenAI gpt-4.x/4o/gpt-5 tiers at
$0/2{,}117$ and $0/1{,}311$) we log zero fabrications. Open-weight
families are not: Qwen3, Qwen2.5, and Llama fabricate at up to
$\mathbf{10.4\%}$, and $70\%$ of those calls name execution-ready
\emph{state-changing} actions such as fabricated \texttt{place\_order} and
\texttt{cancel\_booking} calls, so under a permissive executor the
failure is an unintended \emph{action}, not a failed lookup. We propose
Registry-Visible Reprompting (RVR), a training-free middleware that, on a
registry miss, returns one re-prompt wrapping a structured
\texttt{tool\_not\_found} envelope; it removes all twenty pooled Qwen3
fabrications (Fisher's exact, $p=3.5\!\times\!10^{-5}$ on $20/1{,}417$
vs.\ $0/945$). A powered ablation locates the fix: a \emph{wrong}
registry list recovers as well as the real one (Fisher $p=1.0$), so
recovery is driven by the rejection's format, not the registry's content.
We release the harness, traces, and per-call labels.
\end{abstract}
